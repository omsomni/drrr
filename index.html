BURNS

Burns can be classified in different various types. These degrees depend on how deeply and severely they damaged the skin's surface.
There are 3 degrees of burn: First-degree, Second-degree, Third-degree.
A person may classify a burn immediately when it happens, but it will not be reliable.
It needs to be medically examined in order to see how the burn affected the person's skin.
It can also progress overtime, so it may not be the same as before.

FIRST-DEGREE BURN

First-degree burns usually affect only the outer layer of the skin. which is the epidermis. The burn site is red, painful, dry and has no blisters.

SECOND-DEGREE BURN

Second-degree burns also involves the epidermis and a part of the lower layer of skin, which is the dermis. The burn site looks red, has blisters, and it can be swollen and painful.

THIRD-DEGREE BURN

Third-degree burns, like the first and second degrees, it damages the epidermis and dermis. However, third-degree burns destroy them and may go into the innermost layer of the skin, the subcutaneous tissue. The burn site may look white or blackened due to the damage.

WHAT TO DO?

Fire can occur at any time. As a community, we should be informed and stay alert all the time.
Almost everything and everywhere, there is a fire hazard. We can never be sure that we are safe from fire.
Therefore, we should know what to do before, during, and after a fire broke out.

BEFORE A FIRE


  • 1. Install smoke alarms.
  • 2. Teach children (if any) what smoke alarms are.
  • 3. Let every family member know two ways to escape from every room.
  • 4. Every household member should know the family meeting spot outside the house.
  • 5. Create a family emergency communications plan.
  • 6. Make sure to know the emergency hotlines.
  • 7. Household members should know the STOP, DROP, and ROLL.

DURING A FIRE


  • 1. Understand how fire extinguishers work and use it properly.
  • 2. Remember to get out, stay out, and immediately call your local emergency phone number.
  • 3. You can buy everything again, except your life.
  • 4. Do not use elevators, use the stairs instead.
  • 5. Since you are familiar with the escape routes of your home, if the primary escape route has problems, use your second way out.
  • 6. If there is a need to escape through smoke, get low and go under the smoke.
  • 7. If there is something blocking all the exit routes, stay inside the room and close the doors. Create something to let everyone outside know you need help.
  • 8. Follow your emergency communication plan.

AFTER A FIRE


  • 1. Ask or provide first-aid to anyone who needs it. Cool and cover the burns to reduce the injury or infection it may bring.
  • 2. Let your family know you are safe.
  • 3. If there are any person or animal that are seriously injured should be brought to the hospital immediately.
  • 4. Do not enter homes that are damaged with fire until the authorities say it is safe to enter.

PREVENTION

Knowing that fire can be extremely dangerous, we can follow different actions to reduce the chance of fire.
The Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP) recommends some tips on how to prevent possible fires that can occur at home.

  • 1. Never leave a hot or even a warm hot iron unattended.
  • 2. Never leave food on a hot stove or in an oven unattended. Cooking areas are prone to fire the most, therefore flammable objects should be kept away from this area.
  • 3. Frequently test smoke alarms to make sure they are functioning properly.
  • 4. Burning candles should never be left unattended.
  • 5. Flammable or sources of flame should be stored in a safe area.
  • 6. Space heaters should be kept away from other flammable items.
  • 7. Avoid plugging multiple appliances into an extension cord.
  • 8. Do not put an electrical cord under an inflammable object.
  • 9. Replace defective electronic appliances immediately.
  • 10. Have electrical connections checked once every two years by a licensed electrician.
